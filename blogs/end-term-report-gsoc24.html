End-Term Report - GSoC24

3 min. read Date Fri 20 September 2024 Tags GSoC / Machine Learning / Quantum Computing

In this post, I will share my final progress and outcomes from the GSoC24 project Quantum Contrastive Representation Learning for High Energy Physics in ML4Sci organization. Over the second phase of this project, I explored deeper into the topic of contrastive representation learning, investigating multiple approaches to improve both classical and quantum models. This involved a combination of supervised, unsupervised, hybrid, and fully quantum models, alongside developing new backbones and pipelines for the given datasets.

1. Approach One: Supervised and Unsupervised Contrastive Learning Losses

During this phase, I further investigated the loss functions used in both supervised and unsupervised contrastive learning (CL). This exploration extended to both full quantum and hybrid models. The key focus was to evaluate how well different loss functions, such as temperature-scaled losses like InfoNCE and pairwise contrastive losses, could perform when integrated into hybrid classical-quantum networks. In particular, I analyzed how these loss functions influence the representations learned by classical models versus those augmented by quantum circuits.

Key Findings: Loss functions in the quantum setting, particularly with quantum fidelity loss, showed promising results, although challenges remain in balancing the computational overhead.

2. Approach Two: Graph Processing for Quark-Gluon Jet Images

For the Quark-Gluon Jet (QJ) images, I moved beyond convolutional approaches and began exploring graph-based models to better capture the underlying structure of the data. A key development here was the hybrid quantum model, where I combined classical graph neural networks (GNNs) with quantum layers. This allowed me to leverage quantum computational power for part of the pipeline while keeping the rest of the model classically interpretable. The graph-based approach also enabled improved processing of the sparse and high-dimensional nature of the QJ data, particularly in understanding the geometric patterns within the jets.

Key Findings: The hybrid model showed an improvement in capturing finer details in the QJ data, though optimization of the quantum circuits is needed to scale these methods effectively.

3. Approach Three: Model-Based Augmentation for Unsupervised Contrastive Representation Learning

In the final approach, I focused on model-based augmentations for unsupervised contrastive representation learning. This included creating synthetic data for the Quark-Gluon Jet binary classification task and experimenting with the Quantum Machine 7 (QM7) molecule dataset for a formation energy regression task. Model-based augmentation allowed me to generate more varied samples and better utilize the unsupervised setting, where direct label supervision is unavailable. The rationale behind this approach was to develop a robust representation learning pipeline that could generalize well across different datasets, from high-energy physics data to molecular datasets.

The QM7 dataset consists of 7,165 molecules, with each molecule represented by a set of atomic coordinates and its corresponding total energy, derived from quantum mechanical calculations. This dataset is commonly used for predicting molecular properties such as formation energy, making it an excellent benchmark for exploring how quantum and classical models handle regression tasks. The goal here was to use contrastive learning to extract useful representations of molecules, which could then be used to predict their formation energy. This task also tested the applicability of quantum contrastive learning beyond classification tasks and into regression.

Key Findings: Synthetic data generation significantly boosted the performance in the unsupervised tasks. For QM7, the regression task provided a good test case for evaluating the effectiveness of quantum models in non-classification tasks.

4. Conclusion and Future Work

Throughout this project, I made significant progress in understanding and applying contrastive representation learning in both classical and quantum contexts. The integration of quantum circuits, especially in hybrid models, showed potential but also highlighted areas that require further refinement, particularly in terms of scalability and computational cost. Moving forward, the next steps would involve optimizing these quantum models and investigating more efficient methods of data augmentation and loss function integration.
